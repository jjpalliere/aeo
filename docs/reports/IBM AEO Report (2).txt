AEO Competitive Research Report
2.1 Channels
The citation data across all three competitors surfaces six channels with meaningful AEO relevance: owned site, LinkedIn, World Economic Forum, Wikipedia, third-party publications (Gartner, Deloitte, BCG, HBR, MIT Sloan), and financial media (Yahoo Finance and peer firm sites). Each channel has a distinct content type profile, topic affinity, and AI platform preference.
Owned site
* Who uses it: All three competitors; McKinsey and PwC generate the majority of their citation volume here
* Content types that perform: Long-form reports, downloadable PDFs, FAQ pages, overview and definitional pages, annual surveys
* Topics that surface: AI transformation, organizational strategy, workforce redesign, ESG, financial strategy, cybersecurity
* AI platform preference: Claude favors owned-site long-form content most strongly; Google AI Overviews underperforms on owned-site content that lacks snippet-optimized structure
* IBM implication: The owned site is the primary citation engine, but only when content is structured for AI extraction. Depth alone is insufficient; FAQ pages, definitional content, and clearly headed frameworks are what get cited, not narrative prose
LinkedIn
* Who uses it: PwC most distinctively (citation volume equal to owned site); McKinsey via long-form articles; Signal minimally and only as a subject
* Content types that perform: Long-form LinkedIn articles (not short posts); percentage-led statistical claims; survey data callouts; numbered list formats
* Topics that surface: CEO agenda, AI strategy, workforce and talent, geopolitical uncertainty, agile transformation frameworks
* AI platform preference: Google Snippets picks up LinkedIn content almost exclusively; McKinsey's LinkedIn articles surface on Google but not on Claude or ChatGPT
* IBM implication: LinkedIn is a Google Snippets play specifically, not a broad AI visibility play. The content format that performs (long-form articles with data-forward claims) is distinct from standard social posting. Statistical callouts and percentage-led statements appear to be independently extractable, suggesting IBM IBV should treat LinkedIn articles as structured data assets rather than editorial content
World Economic Forum
* Who uses it: PwC and Microsoft Signal adjacently; McKinsey less so
* Content types that perform: Global outlook reports, cybersecurity summaries, AI transformation briefings, workforce strategy pieces
* Topics that surface: Global risk, AI enterprise adoption, cybersecurity, C-suite leadership through uncertainty
* AI platform preference: Surfaces across platforms without a strong single-platform preference; functions as a credibility amplifier rather than a primary citation source
* IBM implication: WE Forum is a third-party credibility channel. Publishing or syndicating content through WE Forum-adjacent vehicles embeds IBM IBV in the authority clusters AI models treat as consensus sources. This is an infrastructure play, not a volume play
Wikipedia
* Who uses it: McKinsey surfaces here through third-party reference pages (not self-authored content); PwC and Signal do not appear meaningfully
* Content types that perform: Institutional reference pages listing authoritative sources in a category (e.g., "leading management journals," "top consulting firms")
* Topics that surface: Management consulting, business strategy publications, organizational transformation
* AI platform preference: Almost exclusively ChatGPT; Wikipedia-sourced citations are rare on Claude and Google AI Overviews
* IBM implication: Wikipedia is a ChatGPT-specific channel and functions as a category membership signal rather than a content channel. IBM IBV appearing on Wikipedia reference pages (as a named thought leadership program in the relevant categories) would improve ChatGPT visibility specifically. This is a low-effort, high-leverage infrastructure action
Third-party publications (Gartner, Deloitte, BCG, HBR, MIT Sloan)
* Who uses it: McKinsey benefits most from this cluster through embedded citation authority; Signal attempts to enter it but has not yet succeeded; PwC appears in it peripherally
* Content types that perform: Technology trend reports, research citations, comparative analyses, industry benchmarks
* Topics that surface: AI hype cycles, emerging technology trends, strategic planning frameworks, digital transformation
* AI platform preference: Distributed across platforms; these sources function as consensus anchors that all AI platforms draw from
* IBM implication: Breaking into this citation cluster is the single highest-leverage AEO action available to IBM IBV. AI models treat HBR, Gartner, and MIT Sloan as default authorities for executive intelligence; being cited by or alongside these institutions embeds IBM IBV in the consensus layer. This requires external placement and citation-building, not just owned content production
Financial and peer media (Yahoo Finance, Deloitte, EY, KPMG sites)
* Who uses it: PwC primarily, through comparative and competitive context
* Content types that perform: CEO survey data, financial outlook reports, competitive benchmarking content
* Topics that surface: Economic uncertainty, CFO strategy, M&A, regulatory risk
* AI platform preference: Mixed; surfaces across platforms in competitive-set responses rather than as primary citations
* IBM implication: Limited direct relevance for IBM IBV's editorial mandate, but worth noting that peer firm citation (being referenced alongside or in comparison to McKinsey, PwC, Deloitte) contributes to AI models categorizing IBM IBV as a legitimate member of the thought leadership set
2.2 Personas
The competitor citation data is sparse at the persona level. The three programs analyzed do not consistently target named C-suite roles in ways that produce clean, persona-attributed citation signals. What follows draws primarily on two sources: query patterns in the competitor prompt sets (which reveal what C-suite buyers are actually searching for) and IBM's CXO content deck (which provides detailed persona profiles). Competitor citation data provides directional signal where it exists, but should not be read as a rigorous measurement of persona-level AEO performance.
CEO
* Competitor coverage: Strong. PwC's Global CEO Survey dominates this space; McKinsey covers CEO-level AI strategy and transformation extensively. Both programs treat the CEO as a primary audience.
* Query patterns: CEOs search for AI ROI clarity, board-ready proof points, peer benchmarks, and strategic roadmaps. Queries are broad and strategic rather than functional.
* Gap assessment: Well-covered at the level of AI strategy and transformation. Underserved on the "messy middle" — the operational and cultural complexity of AI execution after the strategic decision has been made. The compound volatility theme (tariffs, geopolitical risk, regulatory flux, AI bubble anxiety) is emerging in IBM's persona data but not well-represented in competitor cited content.
CIO / CTO / Tech Leader
* Competitor coverage: Moderate. McKinsey covers organizational and technology strategy at the CIO level; Gartner and IDC (as third-party citation sources) own the more technical end. Signal targets the CTO but has not yet built citation authority with this persona.
* Query patterns: Technology leaders search for ROI frameworks, governance playbooks, hybrid cloud strategy, and vendor evaluation criteria. Queries are more operational than CEO queries.
* Gap assessment: AI governance and responsible scaling content is in demand and moderately covered. The CTO-specific tension between innovation mandate and short-term ROI pressure is underserved across all three competitors.
CFO
* Competitor coverage: Strong for PwC; moderate for McKinsey. PwC's CFO persona scores are the highest of any persona in the competitive set, driven by ESG, financial strategy, and regulatory risk content.
* Query patterns: CFOs search for finance transformation frameworks, AI-driven forecasting, close cycle automation, and board-ready ROI models.
* Gap assessment: CFO coverage is the most mature of any persona in the competitive landscape. The primary gap is at the intersection of finance transformation and AI governance — CFOs who need to operationalize AI responsibly within finance workflows rather than evaluate it strategically.
COO
* Competitor coverage: Weak across all three competitors. COO-specific content is largely absent from cited material; operational transformation queries tend to surface CEO or CIO content instead.
* Query patterns: COOs search for operational efficiency frameworks, supply chain resilience, workforce productivity, and AI integration into core operations.
* Gap assessment: The COO is the most underserved persona in the competitive citation data. Content addressing the COO's specific mandate — operational orchestration, not just strategic transformation — is a clear white space across McKinsey, PwC, and Signal.
CDO / CAIO
* Competitor coverage: Absent across all three competitors. Neither McKinsey, PwC, nor Signal has meaningful cited content targeting the CDO or CAIO specifically.
* Query patterns: CDOs search for data governance frameworks, lineage and readiness infrastructure, and cross-functional alignment with CIO and CAIO. CAIOs search for AI adoption acceleration, production-ready data pipelines, and shared operating models.
* Gap assessment: The CDO and CAIO represent the clearest white space in the competitive landscape. These are emerging roles with high decision-making authority over AI infrastructure, actively searching for content that addresses their specific accountability gaps, and finding very little.
CHRO
* Competitor coverage: Limited. Workforce and talent content surfaces across McKinsey and PwC but is framed through the CEO or COO lens rather than the CHRO's specific mandate.
* Query patterns: CHROs search for AI adoption in HR workflows, workforce redesign, upskilling strategy, and change management frameworks.
* Gap assessment: The CHRO persona is underserved as a primary audience. Workforce content exists in the competitive citation data but is not structured around the CHRO's specific challenges — the tension between technology-driven transformation and the human side of organizational change.
2.3 Topics
Competitive gaps (low saturation, real demand)
The following topics show meaningful query volume in the prompt set but low citation density from the three competitors analyzed:
* CDO/CAIO accountability and operating model alignment — a high-demand topic for an emerging executive cohort with no clear content authority in the market; queries around data readiness, AI governance accountability, and cross-functional alignment between CDO and CAIO return thin or generic results
* The "messy middle" of AI transformation — the operational and cultural complexity between strategic AI commitment and measurable ROI; competitors address the before (strategy) and the after (proof points) but not the during
* Cross-functional C-suite decision-making on AI — how CEO, CIO, CFO, and COO align on AI investment priorities, governance, and execution; queries in this space return fragmented results that address each role individually rather than the alignment challenge between them
* COO-specific operational AI — how AI integrates into core operations (supply chain, workforce productivity, process automation) from the operator's perspective rather than the technologist's or strategist's
* AI governance for non-technical executives — CFOs, CHROs, and COOs navigating AI risk, compliance, and ethics without deep technical fluency; McKinsey and PwC address governance at the CIO/CTO level but not for the broader C-suite
* CHRO-led workforce redesign — organizational change, upskilling, and cultural transformation driven by AI adoption; exists at the periphery of competitor content but not as a primary topic with citation authority
Competitive territory (high saturation, competitors established)
The following topics are well-covered by one or more competitors and represent high-effort entry points where IBM IBV would need to produce distinctively structured or data-forward content to gain share of voice:
* State of AI in enterprise — owned by McKinsey; high citation density across platforms; IBM IBV can compete here but needs a differentiated angle (e.g., cross-industry operational data rather than strategy-level analysis)
* AI transformation frameworks — McKinsey and PwC both have strong citation presence; the format (named, structured frameworks) matters as much as the content
* CEO agenda and AI strategy — PwC's Global CEO Survey anchors this space; competitive entry requires either a comparable data asset or a more specific sub-topic angle
* Workforce and talent strategy — covered broadly by McKinsey and PwC; competitive but not impenetrable, particularly for content that addresses specific functional leaders (CHRO, COO) rather than the CEO
* Why transformations fail — McKinsey has high citation density here; a durable topic with established ownership
2.4 Formats
What formats drive AEO visibility
The citation data across all three competitors points to a consistent set of formats that AI models favor for extraction and attribution, ordered here by citation impact:
* [Long] Annual survey and benchmark reports: the highest-leverage format in the data; function as citation anchors across channels and platforms; the key characteristic is longitudinal, proprietary data that no other source can replicate; PwC's Global CEO Survey is the clearest example — it generates citation volume across owned site, LinkedIn, and third-party media simultaneously
* [Short] FAQ pages: discrete question-and-answer structure maps directly to how conversational AI retrieves and presents information; McKinsey's FAQ content surfaces with frequency disproportionate to its volume in the overall content mix; the format is low-cost to produce relative to its AEO return
* [Long] Named, branded frameworks: give AI models a citable, attributable unit of intellectual property; unnamed frameworks do not accumulate citation authority in the same way; the naming convention matters — a framework needs a distinct label to be retrievable as a discrete entity
* [Short] Statistical callouts and percentage-led claims: appear to be indexed and retrieved independently of the longer assets that contain them; short data-forward statements with clear attribution function as a distinct format within larger reports, not just supporting copy
* [Short] Numbered and list-based content structures ("5 priorities," "7 shifts," "top 10 trends"): consistently extractable; AI models can cite a specific item from a list without needing to process the full document; the format signals structure to retrieval systems regardless of topic
* [Medium] Definitional and overview pages: "What is X," "About this program," and category-definition content contributes to AEO visibility by establishing semantic authority for a topic area; less powerful than data assets but important for foundational topic ownership
* [Medium] Long-form LinkedIn articles: distinct from short posts; perform specifically on Google Snippets rather than Claude or ChatGPT; channel-specific AEO value that owned-site content does not replicate, but platform-limited in reach
Technical and semantic requirements
The following structural characteristics determine whether content — regardless of editorial format — gets retrieved by AI:
* Schema markup (HowTo, FAQ, Article): absent from Signal's content and underutilized by PwC; content structure is partly responsible for McKinsey's performance advantage, not just domain authority; schema should be implemented across all published assets
* Clear H2 and H3 header architecture: AI retrieval systems use headers to identify and extract discrete content units; prose without header structure is retrieved at lower rates than equivalently authoritative content with clear structural signposting
* Snippet-optimized lead paragraphs: the first 150 words of any article or report page should function as a standalone summary; Google AI Overviews and ChatGPT both prioritize content where the key claim is stated early and clearly
* Hub-and-spoke content architecture: a central pillar page for each major topic area linked to supporting content at multiple depths; signals topical authority to AI retrieval systems and prevents citation volume from dispersing across unconnected pages
