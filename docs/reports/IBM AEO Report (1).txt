AEO Competitive Research Report
1.1 McKinsey Quarterly
Program overview
McKinsey Quarterly is McKinsey & Company's flagship thought leadership publication, targeting senior executives across industries with research and analysis on strategy, organizational transformation, and emerging technology. Content is distributed primarily through the owned site (mckinsey.com), with a free membership model that gathers some assets behind registration but does not operate a hard paywall. The program has been running for decades and carries significant institutional authority. Its core editorial focus has shifted materially toward AI transformation, workforce redesign, and organizational agility over the past two years.
Topics
Topics with the strongest citation density across the prompt set:
* State of AI in enterprise
* Why transformations fail
* Agile transformation frameworks
* Hybrid work and workforce redesign
* Leading through organizational change
* Generative AI alignment with business goals
The AI transformation cluster — particularly content addressing implementation challenges rather than hype — is the highest-performing topic area. The combination of topic relevance and format structure appears to drive citation performance rather than topic alone.[a][b]
AEO visibility posture
McKinsey Quarterly is the strongest AEO performer of the three competitors analyzed, surfacing consistently across awareness, consideration, and decision-stage queries. Platform-level performance is uneven:
* Claude: strongest platform; McKinsey's long-form analytical content performs best here, favored by Claude's preference for nuanced, authoritative narrative
* ChatGPT: moderate performance; draws heavily on Wikipedia as a proxy for McKinsey-adjacent content, meaning McKinsey surfaces partly through third-party reference rather than direct citation
* Google AI Overviews: weakest platform, estimated at 37% visibility; McKinsey's content is too dense and context-heavy for the concise snippet extraction that Google's retrieval model prioritizes
Channels
* Owned site: primary citation source across the prompt set; dominance most pronounced on Claude
* LinkedIn: secondary channel, driven primarily by long-form LinkedIn articles (not short-form posts) on topics including generative AI frameworks, agile transformation, and geopolitical uncertainty; picked up almost exclusively by Google Snippets
* Wikipedia: tertiary channel, appearing primarily in ChatGPT responses; the pages driving citations are not McKinsey-authored but reference McKinsey alongside peer institutions (HBR, MIT Sloan, BCG), indicating McKinsey benefits from embedded membership in authoritative knowledge clusters even when it isn't the direct source
Formats
* Long-form analytical reports and downloadable PDFs: core citation drivers across the owned site
* FAQ pages: notable outlier; explicitly structured for AI extraction, providing discrete attributable answers to specific questions, and surfaces with frequency disproportionate to its volume in McKinsey's overall content mix
* Overview and "About" pages: definitional and program-description content contributes to AEO visibility in ways that purely editorial content does not
* LinkedIn articles: platform-native long-form content performs on Google Snippets specifically, indicating channel-specific AEO value distinct from the owned site
Gaps
1. Google AI Overviews visibility is materially lower than performance on Claude and ChatGPT, driven by content structure that prioritizes depth over extractability
2. The rapid-briefing use case — executives seeking a concise synthesized answer rather than a deep-dive — is underserved by McKinsey's current format mix, creating a gap on platforms that reward snippet-ready content
3. Mid-market and growth-stage CEO persona is a relative gap; McKinsey's content skews toward large enterprise and citation density drops for queries specific to mid-cap strategic planning
4. CDO and CAIO personas are largely absent from McKinsey's cited content, reflecting a broader market gap rather than a McKinsey-specific failure
1.2 PwC C-Suite Insights
Program overview
PwC's C-Suite Insights program is the thought leadership arm of PwC's advisory practice, targeting senior executives — primarily CEOs and CFOs — with research on business strategy, ESG, digital transformation, and risk. Content is distributed through the owned site (pwc.com), LinkedIn, and syndicated through third-party business and financial media. The program's flagship asset is the Global CEO Survey, an annual study that generates significant citation volume across channels. Unlike McKinsey Quarterly, PwC's program is more closely tied to its commercial advisory identity, and the content mix reflects that — a higher proportion of survey-driven data assets relative to narrative thought leadership.
AEO visibility posture
PwC performs unevenly across AI platforms:
* Gemini: strongest platform; long-form thought leadership content is indexed reasonably well
* Google AI Overviews: significant underperformance; visibility estimated at 54% with a mention rate of only 50%, indicating content exists in AI training data but is not structured for real-time retrieval
* ChatGPT: moderate; PwC surfaces in competitive-set responses (queries about Big Four firms broadly) but rarely as a primary standalone recommendation
* Overall positioning: average position sits in the mid-tier range across platforms — consistently identified as an option rather than the default authority on any specific topic
Channels
* Owned site: primary citation source, consistent with peer competitors
* LinkedIn: citation volume roughly equal to the owned site — an unusual finding relative to McKinsey and Signal; content driving citations skews heavily toward survey data and executive statistics, with percentage-led claims ("56% of CEOs say...") appearing particularly extractable by AI
* World Economic Forum: meaningful third-party channel; PwC-adjacent content on AI transformation, cybersecurity, and workforce strategy surfaces in WeForum citations
* Yahoo Finance and peer firm sites (Deloitte, EY, KPMG): generate citation volume primarily through comparative and competitive context
Topics
* CEO agenda and AI strategy: strongest topic area, driven by the Global CEO Survey and its annual derivatives
* ESG reporting, financial strategy, and regulatory risk: performs well for CFO-focused queries
* Agentic AI and enterprise adoption
* Talent and workforce strategy
* Risk management: C-suite leadership through uncertainty
* Self-ranking content ("top-ranked firm for X"): surfaces in citations in a way that suggests explicit authority claims, when indexed on credible channels, contribute to AEO positioning
Formats
* Annual survey reports: highest-performing format; the Global CEO Survey functions as a citation anchor across channels and platforms
* Numbered and list-based structures ("7 key shifts," "5 strategic priorities"): perform consistently; flagged in the analysis as strong AEO formats, likely because they are easy for AI to extract and attribute
* Statistical callouts and percentage-led claims: a sub-format worth distinguishing — short data-forward statements appear to be indexed and retrieved independently of the longer reports that contain them
* Self-ranking and superlative content: surfaces in citations in a way that suggests AI models use explicit authority signals when determining recommendation order
Gaps
1. Business Reinvention positioning — a stated brand pillar — has low AEO visibility; AI models do not associate PwC with operational transformation frameworks the way they associate Deloitte
2. CEO persona underperforms relative to CFO; PwC's AI-generated positioning skews toward compliance and tax rather than strategic foresight, limiting citation relevance for high-level strategy queries
3. CDO and CAIO personas are absent from PwC's cited content entirely
4. A 22-point visibility gap between Gemini and Google AI Overviews suggests a structural content formatting issue — content performing in one retrieval environment is not structured for another
1.3 Microsoft Signal
Program overview
Microsoft Signal is a recently launched executive thought leadership magazine targeting senior technology leaders with content on AI strategy, enterprise technology, corporate trust, and platform thinking. It is distributed in both print and digital formats. As a new program with limited publication history, Signal has not yet established the citation infrastructure — owned content depth, third-party links, authoritative cluster membership — that drives AEO visibility. The program's editorial ambition is clear; its AEO presence is not yet commensurate with that ambition.
AEO visibility posture
Signal is the weakest AEO performer of the three competitors, though the nature of that weakness is structural rather than editorial:
* Claude: occasional surface in technical deep-dive scenarios, but not as a primary authority
* ChatGPT: effectively 0% visibility
* Gemini: effectively 0% visibility
* Overall: estimated 1% visibility; Signal is present in AI responses as a subject — referenced in posts about its launch and in comparative content about executive publications — but absent as a cited source of intelligence. Approximately 92 citations in the analysis came from a source that could not be inspected for page-level detail, meaning Signal's actual citation footprint may be slightly broader than the inspectable data suggests, but not materially so
Channels
* Owned site: minimal direct citations
* LinkedIn: launch announcement and comparative posts about executive publications surface Signal as a subject, not a source
* Gartner, McKinsey, Deloitte: Signal appears in competitive comparison contexts through these channels rather than generating its own citation authority
* WE Forum: AI and enterprise adjacency produces some peripheral citation signal
* IBM-owned content (Think newsletter, IBV trend reports, technology outlook pieces): surfaces more frequently in the Microsoft-adjacent citation data than Signal itself — a notable finding given that IBM and Microsoft are operating in adjacent topic spaces
Topics
The topics Signal is attempting to own are well-represented in AI-generated responses, but through other sources:
* AI strategy and enterprise technology transformation — high demand, currently owned by McKinsey, Gartner, and HBR
* Cybersecurity and AI enterprise adoption — the topic areas where Signal-adjacent content surfaces most frequently, driven by third-party citations rather than Signal's own articles
* Corporate trust and platform thinking — limited citation signal in the prompt set tested
Formats
* Trend-reporting and narrative journalism: Signal's apparent editorial mode and the format least favored by AI retrieval systems, which prioritize structured extractable attributable content over editorial prose
* Definitional content, extractable frameworks, FAQ pages, longitudinal data studies: absent or underdeveloped in Signal's current content mix — these are the formats that drive AEO citation density
* Schema markup and structured data: absent, compounding the format gap at the technical level; Signal's content is both editorially and technically under-optimized for AI retrieval
Gaps
1. Signal has not broken into the established citation clusters — HBR, MIT Sloan, Gartner, WeForum — that AI models treat as consensus sources for executive intelligence; without presence in those clusters, Signal's own content cannot accumulate the citation authority needed to surface independently
2. Definitional and framework content is absent, leaving AI models with nothing structured to extract and attribute when answering executive strategy queries
3. 0% visibility on ChatGPT and Gemini reflects both a training data gap (program too new) and a content structure gap (formats not optimized for retrieval-augmented generation)
4. No visible AEO infrastructure investment — schema markup, hub-and-spoke content architecture, citation-building across authoritative third parties — has been made, meaning the gap compounds over time rather than closing organically
