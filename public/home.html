01 / What

Brand visibility in AI search

AEO audits how three major AI assistants respond to queries relevant to your brand. It simulates real user questions across awareness, consideration, and decision stages, then extracts every brand mention, ranking position, and citation URL from the responses.

The output is a structured dataset: who gets mentioned, how often, in what position, and with what sources. No guesswork, no opinions — just the raw signal of how AI sees your competitive landscape.

02 / Why

AI is the new search surface

When someone asks an AI for a product recommendation, the response is the entire result page. There are no ten blue links — there is one synthesized answer. Brands that appear capture intent. Brands that don't, disappear.

AEO makes this visible: it quantifies where your brand stands relative to competitors in AI-generated responses, tracks how that position shifts across funnel stages and buyer personas, and identifies the content gaps that determine who gets cited.

03 / How

Audit workflow

Strategy Brief
92% of AI-summarized searches never result in a click.
LLMs are being asked questions about your brand every day. AEO is how you understand what they're saying — and influence it.
Measuring LLM visibility starts with querying at scale.

There's no platform that shows how an LLM is representing a brand. LLM responses vary — the same question asked twice returns a different answer — so manual spot-checking doesn't produce a reliable picture.

The tool queries programmatically. It submits prompts to each LLM via API and analyzes responses across hundreds of calls: which brands get mentioned, in what rank order, which URLs get cited. At that volume, consistent patterns emerge.

Performance is scored per persona, not just in aggregate.

A CFO evaluating a purchase is asking completely different questions than the team member who'd actually use the product. Each persona has separate LLM interactions, different competitors surfacing, and different sources cited.

Every prompt is mapped to a specific persona. Performance gets scored at the prompt level, then rolled up into a persona-level view showing where a brand is well-represented and where it's absent.

LLMs cite a brand's website, but give more weight to third-party sources.

The channel analysis collects every URL the LLMs cite and classifies each one: owned pages, competitor pages, news outlets, industry sites, review platforms.

Outlets that consistently appear but haven't covered a brand are PR targets. Third-party content that's outdated but still getting surfaced can be corrected. Sources that appear for competitors but not for a brand show where distribution is thin.

How content is structured affects whether LLMs can use it.

Headers written as questions create direct matches to user queries. FAQ sections that restate a question and answer it in bullets mirror the format LLMs use in their own responses. Schema markup and metadata give the LLM an immediate read on what a page is about.

The right prompts come from mapping what a brand covers, what competitors cover, and what buyers need to know.

A brand's website reflects what it has decided to talk about. But buyers ask LLMs who the main players are, how pricing works, and how approaches compare. Most brand websites don't address those questions directly.

The gap analysis maps three things: topics a brand covers, topics competitors cover, and questions buyers are asking. Where those don't overlap is where the content strategy has room to grow.
